Platform Rebuilt.
Results Delivered.

Took FundedNext from a fragmented, outdated dashboard to a cohesive modern platform serving 10,000+ traders — cutting workflow inefficiency by 40%, lifting user engagement by 35%, and eliminating 18.5% of support volume through eight targeted product decisions.

RoleProduct Manager II
CompanyNext Ventures · FundedNext
TimelineApril 2025 — Present
Team20 People (Cross-functional)
DomainFintech · SaaS · Prop Trading
The Challenge

A fragmented platform creating real business risk

FundedNext had three compounding problems: an outdated dashboard driving user frustration, zero real-time risk transparency for traders, and compliance gaps that exposed the platform to regulatory and financial risk.

Friction-heavy flows around KYC, payouts, and a forced 2FA onboarding were generating avoidable support volume and damaging early user retention. There was also no behavioral incentive layer — traders weren't engaging with key platform resources or features beyond their core account activity.

The challenge was systemic. This wasn't a list of features to ship — it required a unified product strategy that addressed compliance, UX, automation, and user motivation simultaneously.

My Approach

Eight decisions that moved the platform forward

01

Operational workflows were entirely manual, creating bottlenecks across the team. Architected a full automation agent in n8n with a 20-person cross-functional team — delivering a 40%+ gain in workflow efficiency.

02

The platform had no structured notification layer, leaving traders disconnected from key account events. Shipped a full notification system (backend and frontend) across both CFD and Futures products — lifting user engagement by 35%.

03

An outdated dashboard was creating friction at every critical touchpoint — KYC verification, payouts, and account management. Leading the full dashboard revamp, modernizing flows to reduce drop-off and lower support contact at key stages.

04

Traders had no visibility into their real-time risk exposure, making it impossible to self-correct before breaching limits. Introduced Risk Cards on the Account Overview — giving traders a live view of their risk profile and the direct impact of their trading behavior.

05

Abusive trading behavior was going undetected, creating platform integrity and financial exposure. Built a compliance regulations framework with active monitoring of flagged accounts and enforcement protocols for non-compliant traders.

06

FAQ engagement was passive and plan purchases weren't being driven by in-product moments. Created FNHunt — a gamified coupon-code hunt embedded in the FAQ, turning passive browsing into an active discovery mechanism that directly drove plan purchases.

07

Customer pain points were distributed across the journey with no single owner accountable for the full experience. Built and led the Identity & Investigation Squad — a dedicated cross-functional unit owning end-to-end customer journey diagnosis and continuous improvement.

08

Forcing all users into 2FA at signup was creating onboarding friction and flooding support with avoidable tickets. Redesigned the 2FA flow: removed it from free user onboarding entirely, enforced it for paid users post-KYC or after 14 days — resolving 18.5% of all support tickets.

The Thinking

How I decided what to build — and what to skip

Behind every shipped initiative was a specific signal, a prioritization call, and something that got cut. This is the layer of decision-making that isn't visible in the outcome numbers.

How I found the problems

Discovery started by building visibility that didn't exist yet. I used n8n to pull all support tickets directly from Intercom, then piped that data into Power BI to tag and categorise tickets by issue type and root cause. Once the pipeline was live and connected to the resolution tracking layer — so tickets could be checked post-closure to confirm the fix actually held — the pattern was immediate: roughly 18% of tickets clustered around a single theme, identity and account access. That single insight, surfaced from structured data rather than intuition, dictated the priority order before I'd written a single PRD. The automation opportunity surfaced differently — through a time audit with the ops team. Mapping 5 high-volume manual workflows revealed that none of them required human judgment; they were just bottlenecks waiting to be eliminated.

Why this order — not another

I used an impact/reversibility matrix rather than pure RICE scoring. The automation work ranked first not because it had the highest user-facing impact, but because it was blocking the whole team's velocity — 20 people were slowed by manual handoffs that could be eliminated. Sequencing it first meant everything else could ship faster. The dashboard revamp, despite being the most visible, was deliberately deprioritized in Phase 1. UI changes without first improving the underlying data and notification layer would have just relocated the friction rather than resolved it. Compliance came mid-sequence because it needed the notification infrastructure to function — you can't enforce trading rules if traders have no visibility into their status.

The hardest tradeoff

The 2FA architecture decision was the clearest example of a tradeoff that required pushing back on a stakeholder default. The compliance team's position was universal 2FA for all users — removing it from free user onboarding looked like a security regression. The argument I made was that 18% of support volume was a measurable business cost of that policy, and that the real security requirement was for funded accounts (where real capital is involved), not free users exploring the platform. The resolution was tiered enforcement: no 2FA until KYC completion or 14 days of activity, at which point it's enforced. This preserved the requirement where it actually mattered while eliminating the friction where it was doing no useful work.

How success was measured

Efficiency was measured by tracking task completion time for the 5 highest-volume manual workflows before and after automation, then calculating aggregate time saved per week across the team. The 40%+ figure is the reduction in time-on-task across those workflows, validated over 4 weeks post-launch. Support volume reduction used the n8n-to-Power BI pipeline I'd built for discovery — the same infrastructure that pulled tickets from Intercom now tracked resolution outcomes, so we could confirm whether a closed ticket on the same root cause reopened within 30 days. That post-resolution tracking is what separated the 18.5% figure from a raw ticket count reduction: it measured durable resolution, not just closure. Engagement was tracked through notification open rates and 30-day session return rate against the equivalent pre-launch window.

Reflection

What I'd do differently

I'd have shipped a smaller notification MVP first — just account status events — before building the full notification system. I had strong confidence in the demand signal from user research, and that confidence was justified. But launching all notification categories simultaneously made it genuinely difficult to attribute the 35% engagement lift to specific event types. A phased rollout would have given cleaner data on which notifications were driving returns versus which were noise, and allowed us to tune the system before it reached every active trader. The outcome number would have been the same, but the learning would have been sharper.
