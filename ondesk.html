OnDesk Mobile Enterprise App

Transforming fragmented enterprise tooling into a single mobile-first platform — with AI voice chat, asset management, and IT support unified under one experience.

RoleBusiness Analyst / PM
CompanySave the Children International
Timeline2024 — 2025
Key FeatureAI Voice Chat — First in Org
DomainEnterprise · Mobile · AI-Powered
The Challenge

Four systems, zero mobility

Employees relied on fragmented desktop tools for leave requests, travel bookings, IT support, and asset management. Each lived in a different system, with different interfaces, different logins, and different failure modes.

The lack of a unified mobile experience was hurting productivity and creating IT bottlenecks. Employees in the field — often working in difficult conditions — couldn't access these tools easily without a laptop. The feedback was consistent: the tools don't work for how people actually work.

The organization needed a mobile-first solution that didn't just consolidate the tools, but rethought how employees would actually interact with IT support in a modern, AI-capable world.

My Approach

Unify, mobilize, and introduce AI-first support

01

Led a cross-functional team to design and build the mobile version of OnDesk — translating a desktop-first platform into a genuinely mobile-native experience

02

Introduced AI Voice Chat for automated support — the first AI-powered feature in the organization, enabling employees to resolve IT issues through voice interaction

03

Integrated the IT Support and Management System — ticketing, troubleshooting, and escalation pathways unified into the OnDesk mobile app

04

Implemented ICT Asset Management — employees and IT admins can track, request, and manage organizational IT resources directly from their phones

05

Delivered the full 4-in-1 consolidation: Leave Management, Travel Booking, IT Support, and Asset Management — one app, one login, one experience

The Thinking

How I decided what to build — and what to skip

Consolidating four systems sounds like a scope problem. The real challenge was deciding which problems justified the consolidation in the first place, and what the right unification point was.

How I found the problems

The discovery process was unusual because the feedback already existed — it had just never been consolidated into a product brief. I started by pulling 3 months of IT support ticket data and tagging each ticket by system of origin and issue type. The distribution was revealing: over 60% of tickets were procedural in nature ("how do I submit a leave request", "where do I book travel") rather than technical failures. The tools weren't broken — they were inaccessible in the moments people needed them. Field staff interviews confirmed the pattern: employees weren't using the systems because opening a laptop felt like disproportionate effort for a 2-minute administrative task. That insight — friction, not functionality — defined the entire product brief.

Why IT support got prioritized within the app

Once the decision to consolidate was made, the question became: which system matters most to get right first? I mapped each of the four systems by usage frequency and pain severity on a simple 2x2. Leave management was high frequency and low pain — the existing system worked, people just wanted it on their phone. IT support was lower frequency but asymmetrically high pain. A broken laptop in the field during an active programme meant days of lost work, not hours. That asymmetry drove the decision to prioritize IT support resolution first within the unified experience, and specifically to invest in the AI Voice Chat capability for exactly that use case — field staff with a device problem, standing, not at a desk, needing to resolve it now.

Why voice instead of text chat

The initial proposal was a text-based chatbot — simpler to build, faster to ship, less testing surface. I pushed for voice because the primary use case scenario made text the wrong choice. When someone's laptop has failed in the field, they are typically standing, often outdoors, managing other work simultaneously. A text interface requires both hands and full attention. Voice allows hands-free interaction. The counter-argument was build complexity and a 3-week timeline extension. The case I made to leadership was that a text chatbot optimized for the wrong context would get low adoption — it would feel like another tool that doesn't work for how people actually work. Voice was harder to build but meaningfully better for the actual use case. Leadership approved a scoped pilot for IT support use cases only, which is the right call — prove it in one context before expanding.

How success was defined without a revenue metric

Internal tools have no revenue metric, which means success requires explicit agreement on what a "good outcome" looks like before you launch — not after. I defined three success criteria upfront: reduction in IT support tickets categorized as procedural (baseline from the ticket audit), time-to-resolution for the 5 most common IT issues (measured pre and post), and adoption rate within 90 days of launch across the employee base. The paperless outcome was binary — the paper-based systems were formally retired on the go-live date. With those three signals agreed before build began, post-launch evaluation had a clear frame rather than a post-hoc search for numbers that looked good.

Reflection

What I'd do differently

The AI Voice Chat should have launched as a single-use-case pilot first — just leave request queries — before expanding to IT support. My reasoning for starting with IT support was that it had the highest pain severity. That was correct. But IT support also has the widest variety of edge cases, which required significantly more intent training and testing than we had scoped. A focused leave-request pilot would have given us a functioning, adoptable product 4 weeks earlier while we continued building out the IT support capability. Users who had experienced the voice feature in a simple context would have arrived at the IT support launch with higher trust in the capability. Scope discipline on AI features in particular — start with the narrow win, expand from adoption.
